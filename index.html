Human Walking

Human walking is a cyclical motion

Walking is symmetrical: left leg and right leg

Two phases:

  • double: both feet on the ground
  • single: only one foot on the ground

Simple Inverted Pendulum Dynamics

Support leg is planted on the ground (anchor point). Usually does not move during the step

Swing leg moves from backward position to forward position, and then becomes the next support leg

Leg consists of thigh and calf, but during walking the knee is almost completely extended

Model the leg as a single straight line

Hip moves around the foot of the support leg like an inverted pendulum. Anchor point is at the bottom.

Equations of Motions

  • \( F = m a \), forces acting on the system are the product of mass multiplied by acceleration
  • Only force acting on the system is gravity, which is split into two components, only the component tangential to the circle that the mass travels on has an impact. The other component is along the string. \[ F = - m g sin \theta = ma \\ a = -g sin\theta \], where \( g \) is gravity, and then \( \theta \) is angle to the vertical

Equations of Motions

  • In the following, it is important that \( \theta \) is expressed as radians
  • Radians: the length of the part of the unit circle (radius = 1.0) that is spanned by \( \theta \)
  • So, in radians, the length of the circle part \( s \) for a circle of radius \( l \) is given as \( s = l \theta \)

    Equations of Motions

    • Differential Equation
      • \[ \frac{d^2\theta}{dt^2} + \frac{g}{l} sin\theta = 0 \]
    • Motion is independent of the mass m of the object

    Equations of Motions: Numerical Solution

    • Analytical solution for this differential equation is not known
    • Numerical solutions can be found by integrating this differential equation
    • Implement a function step(state, dt = 0.01 ) that calculates the next state of the system
    • Definition of a state
      • s = [ theta, theta_d, theta_dd ]
    • Initial value problem
      • initial = [ -30.0 degrees, 0, 0 ]

    T, T_D, T_DD = range(3)

def step( state, dt = 0.01 ):
  ntheta = state[T] + state[T_D] * dt 
  ntheta_d = state[T_D] + state[T_DD] * dt
  ntheta_dd = - g/l * math.sin(state[T] )
  return (ntheta, ntheta_d, ntheta_dd)

    Equations of Motions: Numerical Solution

    • Function can be expressed in matrix form
    • \[ q' = M q \\ q = \left[ \begin{array}{c}\theta\\ \dot{\theta}\\ \ddot{\theta} \end{array} \right] \]

    Equations of Motions: Numerical Solution

    • Function cannot be expressed in matrix form (System is not a linear system)
    • Make the approximation assumption that if \( \theta << 1 \), then \( \theta \approx \sin \theta \). Assume \( \theta \) < 10 deg.
    • \[ q' = M q \\ q = \left[ \begin{array}{c}\theta\\ \dot{\theta}\\ \ddot{\theta} \end{array} \right] \\ q' = \left[ \begin{array}{c}\theta'\\ \dot{\theta}'\\ \ddot{\theta}' \end{array} \right] = M \left[ \begin{array}{c}\theta\\ \dot{\theta}\\ \ddot{\theta} \end{array} \right] \\ \left[ \begin{array}{c}\theta'\\ \dot{\theta}'\\ \ddot{\theta}' \end{array} \right] = \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & dt & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & dt \\ -g/l & 0 & 0 \\ \end{array}\right] \left[ \begin{array}{c}\theta\\ \dot{\theta}\\ \ddot{\theta} \end{array} \right] \\ \]

    Humanoid Robots

    • Expressing the dynamics of a humanoid robot is also possible in matrix form
    • Matrix is much larger
    • For each joint, we need to record angle, joint velocity, and joint acceleration (force)

    Euler Integration

    • Only integrate using the next derivative as input
    • position = position + vel (derivative of position)
    • velocity = velocity + accel (derivative of velocity)
    • First order approximation
    • In practice, you need to use more sophisticated methods to avoid ever increasing errors.
    • Open Dynamics Engine (ODE) uses Runge-Kutta 4 method, which is based on the Taylor series expansion of the function
    T, T_D, T_DD = range(3)
GRAVITY = 9.81 # m/s**2
LINK_LENGTH = 1.1 # m

def step( state, dt = 0.01 ):
  ntheta = state[T] + state[T_D] * dt 
  ntheta_d = state[T_D] + state[T_DD] * dt
  ntheta_dd = - GRAVITY/LINK_LENGTH * math.sin(state[T] )
  return (ntheta, ntheta_d, ntheta_dd)

    Analysis of the Energy in the System

    • Two energies at play in the pendulum system
      1. Potential energy: energy due to gravity
      2. Kinetic energy: energy due to velocity
    • Energy in the closed system is constant
    • E = E_pot + E_kin is constant

    Analysis of the Energy in the System

    • Potential energy: \( m * g * h \)
    • Kinetic energy: \( \frac{1}{2} * m * v^2 \)

    Solving ordinary differential equations (ODEs)

    • An ordinary differential equation includes only one independent variable that depends on one of several derivates of a function
    • Solving these requires a solid background in numerical computation and mathematics
    • Better to use special purpose libraries
      • Open Dynamic Engine
      • SciPy
      . 
    import scipy
from scipy import integrate

    Solving a system of ODEs

    • \[ \ddot{\theta} + \frac{g}{l} sin\theta = 0 \]
    • This is a 2nd order differential equation, but scipy can only solve first order differential equations
    • Work around by an additional variable \( \theta_2 \), and use \( \theta_1 \) to refer to our original theta
    • \[ \begin{eqnarray} \theta_2 & = & \dot{ \theta_1 } \\ \dot{ \theta_2 } & = & - \frac{g}{l} sin \theta_1 \end{eqnarray} \]
    • Now, we have the equation of motion in a format that scipy can deal with

    Conversion to Python

    • Convert into a python function
    • def pendulum_dynamics( state_x, time )
    • This function will be called integrate.odeint to simulate the behavior of the pendulum

    Improved Pendulum Dynamics

    • Simple dynamics model did not include losses due to friction
    • Extend our model to include this friction by adding a friction loss with coefficient \( b \)
    • \[ \ddot{\theta} + \frac{b}{m}\dot{\theta} +\frac{g}{l} sin\theta = 0 \]

    Inverted Pendulum

    • Inverted pendulum is used to model the movement of the torso over the support leg
    • Extending the leg will slow down the rotation around the pivot point due to the conservation of angular momentum
    • Torque applied at the ankle can move the pivot point forward/backward on the foot, such changing the direction of the gravity force applied
